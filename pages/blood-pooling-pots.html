Blood Pooling Is NOT Caused by "Loose Veins"

The idea that blood pools in your legs because your veins are too stretchy is everywhere. But the actual mechanism is more interesting — and more treatable — than that.

Dysautonomia

Dr. Nathan Keiser

If you've spent any time in the POTS or dysautonomia world, you've probably heard the explanation: your blood pools in your legs because your veins are too loose, too stretchy, too floppy. Maybe someone mentioned EDS in the same breath. The image you get is of these saggy tubes that can't hold blood up against gravity.

And sure, that could be a thing. But for most people with blood pooling, that's not actually what's happening.

When Veins Really Are the Problem

OK so let's start with the scenario where the venous system actually is the culprit. If all the tension in the veins just relaxes and blood can't get back up to the heart, you'd notice it. You'd have blown-out veins, varicosities, everything dropping into the legs — and they're usually quite painful. That's its own thing, right?

A more subtle version would be veins that are just kind of hypo-functioning. That can happen too. But here's where it gets interesting: even that is going to be driven in a large way by the arterial outputs. By the brain's control of the system.

So the question isn't really "are my veins loose?" The question is: why isn't my brain controlling blood distribution properly?

Your Brain Has a Map of Your Body

If you have a picture of your body in your mind — your brain actually has a map of every part of your body. It's in an area called the primary sensory cortex, and it's called the homunculus. It sits kind of right on top of your head and then hooks down in the middle between the hemispheres, like an M shape going around. The left side of your head feels the right side of your body, and vice versa.

So any point that you touch on your skin, anything that's a sensation in your muscles, anything that moves in your joints — all of that has a little dot on this map, and those dots are all communicating with your brain constantly.

Here's where blood pooling comes in.

The Same Map That Controls Your Muscles Controls Your Blood Vessels

The same map of your brain that controls the output of, say, your leg is also going to control the blood vessels that serve that leg. They come from the same area in the brain. So if you've got a muscle that doesn't work accurately, the probability that the vessel controlling blood flow to that area doesn't work accurately is high — because they're correlated in your brain.

Let's say, for example, you don't have great sensation in your leg below the knee on one side. And you also have some movement troubles there — you're weak, you're uncoordinated. Like, you go to tap your foot on the floor in a rhythm and it's hard to catch a beat. If the sensation is bad and the motor control is off, the vascular control for that same territory is probably off too.

The key insight: When we think about a muscle in the body not working effectively, we should think about the muscles of the arterial system not working effectively within the same brain map. Blood flow control and movement control are correlated — they share the same neural real estate.

And it doesn't have to be your whole leg. It can be little patches of areas. It's not like it's dead — it's more nuanced than that.

Three Ways Blood Flow Control Goes Wrong

When we look at how blood flow distribution breaks down, we think about the same three variables we use for any movement disorder:

  • Latency: The response time to get blood where it needs to go may be slow. The signal arrives, but it's late.
  • Accuracy: Maybe the body needs blood in the lower leg, but it gets to the thigh instead. It's not accurate to the specific area.
  • Amplitude: The volume. Is the response big enough? Is enough blood actually getting moved?

So when we have pooling — whether it's in your gut, your legs, your arms, wherever — we're trying to understand: is it because peripherally the blood supply is slow, inaccurate, or not enough? Is the return to the heart getting delayed because of those errors? Or is it something else entirely?

It's Not Always Peripheral

Here's the other possibility that gets missed. Sometimes, neck down, everything runs great. The peripheral vascular system is fine. But the arterial systems going into the head are getting blocked or restricted. That changes outputs too.

Or the baroreceptors — the sensors that measure how much blood is in your head — are getting it wrong. They're assuming you have more blood in your head than you actually do, or less. And the autoregulation, the brain's ability to manage that pressure across position changes, is decayed. So when you lay down, a ton of pressure rushes in, and when you stand up, a ton of pressure rushes out. Like the tide rolling in and out.

All of those are different mechanisms. All of them cause pooling. And none of them are "your veins are too stretchy."

Recalibrating the Map

So when we talk about recalibrating the nervous system — and that is a bit of a cliche way to say it — what we really mean is we have to improve the functionality of the map. The sensory map, the motor map, and how they talk to each other.

We go back to that model: central sensory, central motor, peripheral motor, peripheral sensory. If the output is bad — arteries aren't responding well, blood isn't getting to the head — then we back it out. Is the brain not processing correctly? Are we looking at movement disorders in the same territory? And then how does that relate to the information coming in? Is there a peripheral neuropathy that's the main problem?

Sometimes, especially in post-infectious patients, the peripheral nerves just don't work. We have to figure that out. But a lot of times, it's processing in the brain where the maps aren't accurate. And if the map's not accurate, when you go to navigate off the map, the navigation's poor. You get a bad output.

What about IV saline for pooling? If blood pooling is driven by inaccurate brain maps and poor vascular control — not by "loose veins" — then adding more fluid volume doesn't fix the underlying mechanism. The brain still doesn't know how to distribute it properly. You've added more water to the system, but the pump directing it is still miscalibrated.

Your Brain Is Built to Get Better at This

Here's what's cool about the brain. We don't give it enough credit — the whole thing is designed to just always get better at processing the things you're doing. The more time you spend doing anything, the better you get at understanding how to do it more efficiently. People can get good at weird stuff, right? You see Guinness World Record stuff and you're like, "How did you spend that much time?" But people get good at juggling chainsaws, spinning plates, playing basketball, writing code — whatever you spend a lot of time on, your brain learns how to do more efficiently.

Where we run into trouble is when something has created a problem — an injury, an illness — and you're trying to do something that's way above the dose level of what you're currently capable of. Your brain can't make that leap.

So we break it down into segments that are easier to achieve. Build those segmental capacities, then stack them back up again. It sounds really simple, but it's just how learning works. If you were going to learn calculus, you'd start with addition and subtraction, then multiplication, then long division, then algebra, then geometry, and you'd work your way up. You don't just start trying to do calculus.

Same concept. If the brain is stuck and it can't get blood flow distribution right, we break the problem down into simpler chunks — sensory input, motor output, vascular control — and build it back up again.
