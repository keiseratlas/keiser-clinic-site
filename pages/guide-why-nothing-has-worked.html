Why Nothing Has Worked Yet

The 4 mechanisms your doctors aren't testing for — and how to find which one is keeping you sick

You've done everything right. You drank more water. You added salt. You wore the compression stockings. You tried the medications. You rested when they said rest. You pushed through when they said push through.

And you're still sick.

Your tests came back "normal." Your doctor said "it's anxiety." Your cardiologist said "your heart is fine." Your neurologist shrugged. And somewhere in there, you started wondering if maybe they're right — maybe it IS all in your head.

It's not. The reason nothing has worked is that nobody has looked for the actual mechanism driving your symptoms. They've been treating what they can see — heart rate, blood pressure, anxiety — without asking WHY those things are happening.

Your Diagnosis Is a Description, Not an Explanation

POTS tells you your heart rate goes up 30 beats when you stand. It doesn't tell you WHY.

Dysautonomia tells you your autonomic nervous system isn't working right. It doesn't tell you WHAT'S causing it.

"Vestibular dysfunction" tells you something is off with balance. It doesn't tell you WHERE the problem actually is.

These are descriptions. They describe what's happening. They don't explain what's DRIVING it.

Two patients with identical POTS diagnoses can have completely opposite mechanisms — and need completely opposite treatments. That's why the same protocol helps one person and does nothing for another.

Until you know your mechanism, you're guessing. And guessing is why nothing has worked.

Mechanism #1: Your Brain Isn't Getting Enough Blood

What's happening

When you stand up, blood flow to your brain decreases more than it should. Your heart races to try to compensate. This isn't a heart problem — it's a blood delivery problem.

How we find it

Transcranial Doppler (TCD) during tilt testing. We watch blood velocity in your middle cerebral artery in real time as you change position. Not just heart rate — actual blood flow.

What it looks like

Blood flow drops 20-40% within minutes of standing. Heart rate spikes to compensate. Symptoms get worse when you try to think and stand at the same time.

What helps

Volume expansion (salt, water, compression) provides temporary support. Horizontal cardiovascular training builds capacity without crashing you. Progressive tilt training retrains your autonomic reflexes over time.

What doesn't help

Vestibular exercises alone — wrong mechanism. Medications that slow heart rate — that's removing your body's compensation, not fixing the problem.

Mechanism #2: Your Vestibular System Is Sending False Signals

What's happening

Your inner ear is telling your brain you're moving when you're not (or the other way around). Your autonomic system responds to a threat that doesn't exist. This creates a cascade — dizziness, nausea, heart rate changes, anxiety — all from a signal problem.

How we find it

VNG (videonystagmography) with caloric, rotational, and positional testing. We challenge each vestibular pathway and watch how your eyes respond. The eyes don't lie — they reveal exactly where the signal is breaking down.

What it looks like

Asymmetric vestibular function. Abnormal VOR (vestibulo-ocular reflex). Nystagmus in certain positions. Symptoms worse in visually busy environments like grocery stores or scrolling on your phone.

What helps

Targeted vestibular rehabilitation matched to your specific dysfunction. Not generic balance exercises — specific drills for your specific problem.

What doesn't help

Salt loading — wrong mechanism entirely. Generic "balance exercises" not matched to your testing results.

Mechanism #3: Your Cerebellum Can't Calibrate Properly

What's happening

The cerebellum is your brain's calibrator. It times everything — eye movements, blood pressure adjustments, reflexes, coordination. When it can't calibrate correctly, everything is slightly off. Not broken — just miscalibrated.

How we find it

Detailed oculomotor testing. We measure saccades (fast eye jumps), smooth pursuit (tracking), and gaze stability. Abnormal patterns reveal cerebellar timing dysfunction that no standard exam looks for.

What it looks like

Eyes overshoot or undershoot targets. Blood pressure adjustments are too slow or overshoot then crash. Multiple "unrelated" symptoms — dizziness, brain fog, coordination problems, exercise intolerance — that all started around the same time.

What helps

Cerebellar rehabilitation — oculomotor drills, timing exercises, dual-task challenges. Precision work, not just general exercise.

What doesn't help

Standard vestibular PT — it's targeting the wrong structure. Medications that don't address calibration.

Mechanism #4: Your Neck Is Affecting Blood Delivery

What's happening

Your cervical spine contains vertebral arteries and proprioceptive pathways. Instability or dysfunction here can affect blood flow to the brainstem AND send incorrect position signals to your brain. This is one of the most commonly missed contributors.

How we find it

Digital Motion X-ray (DMX) captures your cervical spine while you actually move. Static MRI misses dynamic problems — it only shows you at rest. Vascular testing may reveal positional flow changes that only happen in certain head positions.

What it looks like

Symptoms get worse in certain head positions. History of whiplash or neck trauma — even "minor" stuff you barely remember. Normal MRI but persistent symptoms that nobody can explain.

What helps

Cervical stabilization if instability is present. Proprioceptive retraining. Addressing the mechanical contributor that keeps re-triggering everything downstream.

What doesn't help

Ignoring the neck because the MRI was "normal." Treating only the downstream symptoms without looking at what's feeding them.

These Mechanisms Overlap

Most patients don't have just one mechanism. They have two, three, or all four interacting at the same time.

  • Blood flow problems cause vestibular symptoms
  • Vestibular problems affect blood pressure regulation
  • Cerebellar dysfunction affects both
  • Neck problems can contribute to all of the above

The question isn't which ONE you have. It's which combination — and which to address first. This is exactly why "try this medication" or "do these exercises" hasn't worked.

How to Start Figuring Out Your Mechanism

Look at what helps — and what doesn't

If This Helps Suspect This Mechanism
Salt / water / compression Blood flow (#1)
Lying down Blood flow + possibly vestibular (#1, #2)
Eyes closed Vestibular mismatch (#2)
Avoiding busy environments Vestibular + cerebellar (#2, #3)
Nothing seems to help Multiple mechanisms or wrong approach

Look at your history

  • Symptoms started after a viral illness — often vestibular + autonomic (#2 + #1)
  • Symptoms started after a concussion — often cerebellar + vestibular (#3 + #2)
  • Symptoms started after neck trauma — consider cervical contribution (#4)
  • Symptoms started gradually, no clear trigger — may be multiple subtle mechanisms

Get proper testing

Not just a tilt table with heart rate. Comprehensive testing means:

  • TCD during tilt (blood flow to the brain)
  • VNG with caloric testing (vestibular function)
  • Oculomotor assessment (cerebellar calibration)
  • Dynamic imaging if cervical contribution is suspected

The Treatment Sequence Matters

Even if you have all 4 mechanisms, you can't treat them all at once. The order matters:

  1. Stabilize blood flow first — You can't do vestibular rehab if standing crashes you
  2. Address vestibular input — Clean up the signals coming in
  3. Retrain cerebellar processing — Improve how the brain uses those signals
  4. Build cardiovascular capacity — Add load once regulation is actually working
  5. Integrate with real-world challenges — Apply it to daily life

Skip steps and you crash or plateau. Right sequence means compounding progress.
